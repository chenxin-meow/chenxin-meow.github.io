A glance at Solow Model of Economic Growth

Posted on 2021-09-07 Edited on 2021-09-12 In Theory
11k 10 mins.

It's widely accepted to measure economic growth by "change in GDP per capita". If we focus on the production side of the economy, the definition of GDP should be how much the domestic economy produces in a year. Admittedly, GDP per capita ignores many aspects of the whole economy within a country (e.g. the black economy, home production, value of various services, etc.), so there are various alternative measures for economic growth, such as the Human Development Index in UN and Index of Economic Well-being in Canada. GDP per capita, however, is still the most widely accepted index to evaluate the level of economic growth. Before we start to analyze the economic growth via GDP, we here to establish a general context of classical macroeconomics.

Setup of Classical Macroeconomics

Given three critical assumptions:

  1. Market clearing
  2. Fixed capital stock at a point in time
  3. Full employment at a point in time

We are interested in both the production and expenditure side of the economy.

Looking into the production side, we want to construct a production function that uses some inputs (capital, labor) to produce some output (goods, services). The formulation of production function is \[ Y=F(K,L) \]

where Y is the final output and K, L are capital and labor respectively. And F represents the available technology for turning the given capital and labor into output.

There are several assumptions on F :

  1. Positive marginal products, i.e. \(F_K>0, F_L>0\).
  2. Marginal products decrease as labor or capital increases, i.e. \(F_{LL}<0, F_{KK}<0\).
  3. Constant returns to scale technology, i.e. \(\lambda F(K,L)=F(\lambda K,\lambda L)\).

Since we assume the capital and labor are fixed, we have \[ Y=F(\bar K, \bar L)=\bar Y \]

Key: In classical macroeconomics, the output is determined by the level of capital and labor stocks in employment.

Now we turn to the expenditure side of the economy. The expenditure on domestic goods can be decomposed into several parts: \[ E=C+I+G+(X-M) \] where

  • \(C=C(Y-T)\) is the consumption,
  • \(I=I(r)\) is the Investment,
  • \(G\) is the Government spending,
  • \(X\) and \(M\) are Exports and Imports respectively.

Given the definitions above, the general equilibrium in classical macroeconomics can be written as \[ \bar Y=E=C+I+G+(X-M) \]

In other words, it is Production = Expenditure.

Under the world of classical macroeconomics, we are going to analyze the economic growth by GDP per capita ( \(Y/N\) ) now.

Note that the international data averaging over a long period of time suggests that

  1. Income per unit capital ( \(Y/K\)) does not change.
  2. Income per capita ( \(Y/N\) ) and capital per capita ( \(K/N\) ) grows at constant rates.
  3. Rental price of capital ( \(r\) ) is constant.
  4. Wages ( \(w\) ) grows at a constant rate.
  5. Rates of growth and level of income vary substantially across countries (non-convergence).

We also call the above five components Kaldor's Stylized Facts of Economic Growth.

Our task is to develop a model on economic growth which can interpret these facts. In this article, I am going to talk about an exogenous growth model, Solow Growth Model, which can be separated into three models, the basic Solow Growth Model, Solow Growth Model with population growth, Solow Growth Model with technology.

Basic Solow Growth Model

Considering 5 assumptions:

A1: Multi-periods.

A2: The production function has a constant returns to scale (CRTS) technology.

For the production function \(Y=F(K,N)\)​, on per-capita basis (letting \(y=Y/N,k=K/N\)), we have \[ y=\frac{Y}{N}=F(\frac{K}{N},1)=f(k) \] A3: No government and no international trade.

Recall that

  • Production = Expenditure, i.e. \(Y=C+I+G+(X-M)\)
  • Disposable Income = Consumption + Savings, i.e. \(Y-T=C+S\)

We derive that \(S-I=(G-T)+(X-M)\), which is an important equation implying Twin Deficits in an economy.

Under the A3 assumption, \(S=I\)​​. Consumers save a fraction ( \(s\): saving rate ) of their income: \[ S=sY=I,\ C=(1-s)Y \] On per-capita basis, \[ sf(k)=i,\ c=(1-s)f(k) \]

A4: All factors of production are fully employed (no unemployment; no capital lies idle).

A5: Capital stock \(K\) can increase because of investment and decrease because of depreciation.

Let \(d\) be the rate of depreciation, \(K, K^{'}\)​​ be the capital stock in the current and next period. Then \[ K'=(1-d)K+sY \] In per-capita form ( assume \(N=N'\)​ ), \[ k'=(1-d)k+sy\Rightarrow k'=k+[sf(k)-dk] \] In long-run equilibrium, all investments are used to replace depreciated capital, so the capital per capita, \(k\)​, should not change, i.e. \(k^*=k=k'\): \[ sf(k^*)=dk^* \]

The basic Solow Model indeed can explain some economic phenomena in the real world. Analyzing the graph, a higher savings rate (hence higher investment) would imply a higher income per capita, which is consistent with the reality. On the other hand, a higher depreciation rate leads to lower income per capita.

Since \(y\)​​​ only depends on \(k\)​​​ ( \(y=f(k)\)​​​ ), \(y\)​​​ does not change in long-run equilibrium as well. This is the major problem of the basic Solow Growth Model -- it contradicts the 2nd Kaldor's Stylized Fact ( \(y,k\)​​​ grow at constant rates). In Solow's world, growth occurs only during adjustment to the steady rate. Nevertheless, it is still a starting point for thet analysis of economic growth. With the establishment of the basic Solow Model, we can now turn to two variational Solow Model, with population or technology growth added.

Solow Growth Model with Population Growth

We have already known, in the equilibrium, capital per capita \(k\)​​​ would reach a steady rate \(k=k'=k^*\)​​​. If we care about the growth rate of \(k\)​​, \(\frac{k'-k}{k}\)​​ should equal 0. More concisely, by the definition of \(k\)​​​, we have \[ \begin{aligned} k=\frac{K}{N}&\Rightarrow lnk=lnK-lnN\\ &\Rightarrow \frac{1}{k}\frac{dk}{dt}=\frac{1}{K}\frac{dK}{dt}-\frac{1}{N}\frac{dN}{dt}\\ &\Rightarrow \frac{k'-k}{k}\approx \frac{K'-K}{K}-\frac{N'-N}{N} \end{aligned} \] Intuitively, when the population grows, the economy requires more capital in order to grow (compared to the situation that the population remains the same).

Denote \[ \frac{N'-N}{N}:=n \]

In previous section, we have already derived \[ K'=(1-d)K+sY \] Hence, \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{k'-k}{k}&= \frac{sY-dK}{K}-n\\ \Rightarrow k'-k&=sf(k)-(n+d)k \end{aligned} \] The last formula is known as Capital Evolution Equation, indicating that for the level of capital stock to grow, investments have to compensate both the extra population and the depreciation of capital.

Again, if we analyze the equilibrium where the capital grows at a steady rate \(k=k'=k^{*}\): \[ sf(k^*)=(n+d)k^* \]

Consider a real-world application: From the graph, a higher population growth rate (\(n_1\to n_2\)​​​​​ ) will cause capital per person to fall (\(k_1 \to k_3\)​​), hence result in lower GDP per person as \(y=f(k)\)​​​​.

How does Solow Growth Model with population growth perform?

On one hand, total output and capital stock grow at the same rate as population growth since \[ \frac{k'-k}{k}= \frac{K'-K}{K}-\frac{N'-N}{N}=0 \] which is an improvement compared to the basic Solow Growth Model.

On the other hand, we still have NO GROWTH in GDP per person as \(y=f(k)\)​ is a function purely relying on \(k\)​. Again, growth occurs only during adjustment to the steady rate.

We still need to add more variations under the framework, here comes the Solow Growth Model with Technology growth.

Solow Growth Model with Technology Growth

Roughly speaking, technology enables higher-level production without needing extra labor or capital. Considering a production function \(Y=F(K,N)\)​​, adding more labor or capital just affects the input of the production function, but the technology improvement can directly change the production function itself.

Let \(A\) denote the state of technology, and \(g=\frac{A'-A}{A}\)​ be the technology growth rate. There are three types of technology progress:

  • Hicks Neutral: \(Y=AF(K,N)\)
  • Labor-Augmenting: \(Y=F(K,AN)\)
  • Capital-Augmenting: \(Y=F(AK,N)\)​

The Solow Model considers Labor-Augmenting technology progress \(Y=F(K,AN)\), so we have \(y=f(k,A)\).

Rather than "per person" argument, we define output per "effective unit of labor" and capital per "effective unit of labor" by \[ \widetilde{y}=\frac{Y}{AN},\ \widetilde{k}=\frac{K}{AN} \] Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\widetilde{k}'-\widetilde{k}}{\widetilde{k}}&= \frac{K'-K}{K}-\frac{A'-A}{A}-\frac{N'-N}{N}\\ \Rightarrow \widetilde{k}'-\widetilde{k}&= \widetilde{k}(\frac{sY-dK}{K}-g-n)\\ \Rightarrow \widetilde{k}'-\widetilde{k}&= sf(\widetilde{k})-(d+g+n)\widetilde{k} \end{aligned} \]

The last equation refers to the new Capital Evolution Equation.

Analyzing the equilibrium, at the steady rate \(\widetilde{k}=\widetilde{k}'=\widetilde{k}^{*}\): \[ sf(\widetilde{k}^*)=(d+g+n)\widetilde{k}^* \] Given the above equation, we can easily tell that the capital per effective worker \(\widetilde{k}\)​​ and the output per effective worker \(\widetilde{y}\)​​ DO NOT grow as \(\widetilde{y}=f(\widetilde{k},A)\)​​. However, if technology grows, the capital per worker \(k\)​​ and the output per worker \(y\)​​​​​​​ DO grow (both at the rate of technology growth).

Verification: Given \[ \widetilde{k}=\frac{K}{AN}=\frac{k}{A}\\ \widetilde{y}=\frac{Y}{AN}=\frac{y}{A}\\ \] Under equilibrium, \[ 0=\frac{\widetilde{k}'-\widetilde{k}}{\widetilde{k}}= \frac{k'-k}{k}-\frac{A'-A}{A}\Rightarrow \frac{k'-k}{k}=g \]

\[ 0=\frac{\widetilde{y}'-\widetilde{y}}{\widetilde{y}}= \frac{y'-y}{y}-\frac{A'-A}{A}\Rightarrow \frac{y'-y}{y}=g \]

This is consistent with Kaldor's Stylized Fact

  • Income per capita ( \(Y/N\) ) and capital per capita ( \(K/N\) ) grows at constant rates.

It is a revolutionary breakthrough because we ultimately observe "economic growth".

Evaluation of Solow Model

Since we are so interested in Kaldor's Stylized Fact, we evaluate our 3 models

  • M1: Basic Solow Model
  • M2: Solow Model with Population Growth
  • M1: Solow Model with Population Growth & Technology Growth

under the framework of 5 Kaldor's Stylized Fact.

  1. Income per unit capital ( \(Y/K\)​​ ​​) does not change.

    This is equivalent to say, \(Y,K\) grow at the same rate.

    • M1 (\(\checkmark\)): \(\frac{K'-K}{K}=\frac{Y'-Y}{Y}=0\)
    • M2 (\(\checkmark\)): \(\frac{K'-K}{K}=\frac{Y'-Y}{Y}=n\)​
    • M3 (\(\checkmark\)​): \(\frac{K'-K}{K}=\frac{Y'-Y}{Y}=n+g\)​

  2. Income per capita ( \(Y/N\)​ ) and capital per capita ( \(K/N\)​ ) grows at constant rates.

    • M1 and M2 (✘): \(\frac{y'-y}{y}=\frac{k'-k}{k}=0\)
    • M3 (\(\checkmark\)): \(\frac{y'-y}{y}=\frac{k'-k}{k}=g\)

  3. Rental price of capital ( \(r\)​​ ) is constant.

    • M1 and M2 and M3 (\(\checkmark\)​)

    Here we consider a special case: assume the production function is in a Cobb-Douglas form \[ Y=K^{\alpha}(AN)^{1-\alpha} \] Then the economy's profit function is \[ \pi=Y-wN-rK-dK=K^{\alpha}(AN)^{1-\alpha}-wN-rK-dK \] Choose \(K,N\) to maximize \(\pi\): \[ \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial K}=\alpha K^{\alpha-1}(AN)^{1-\alpha}-r-d=0\ \ \ \ (1)\\ \] \[ \frac{\partial \pi}{\partial N}=A(1-\alpha) K^{\alpha-1}(AN)^{-\alpha}-w=0\ \ \ \ (2) \]

    From (1), \[ r=\alpha K^{\alpha-1}(AN)^{1-\alpha}-d=\alpha\widetilde{k}^{\alpha-1}-d \] Since \(\widetilde{k}\)​ does not change, \(r\) is constant.

  4. Wages ( \(w\)​​​ ) grows at a constant rate.

    From (2), \[ w=A(1-\alpha) K^{\alpha-1}(AN)^{-\alpha}=A(1-\alpha)\widetilde{k}^{\alpha} \] Since \(\widetilde{k}\) does not change, \[ \frac{w'-w}{w}=\frac{A'-A}{A}=g \]

    • M1 and M2 (✘): \(\frac{w'-w}{w}=0\)
    • M3 (\(\checkmark\)): \(\frac{w'-w}{w}=g\)

  5. Rates of growth and level of income vary substantially across countries (non-convergence).

    • M1 and M2 and M3 (✘): the models predict that all countries should have the same long-run growth rate, \(g\), which is inconsistent with what we observe in reality.

Given our starting point to satisfy Kaldor's Stylized Facts, Solow Model can explain many Stylized Facts of economic growth. In particular, Solow Model with Population Growth & Technology Growth can interpret 4 Kaldor's facts out of the 5.

For the term "economic growth", Solow predicts Growth rate of GDP per capita = Growth rate of Technology, i.e. \[ \frac{y'-y}{y}=g \] But how does the growth rate of technology be determined? This is not explained in Solow Model. In other words, the growth rate of technology, \(g\)​, is an exogenously given value that we cannot formulate from Solow Model itself. This is the reason why we refer Solow Model as an exogenous growth model -- a starting port to analyze economic growth. Of course, there would be some endogenous growth models (e.g. Romer One-Sector Model, Lucas Two-Sector Model, Romer Two-Sector Model), but all of them should credit their work to Solow.

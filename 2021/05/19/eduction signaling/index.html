Why Education? — A modern economist's view

Posted on 2021-05-19 Edited on 2021-05-20 In Theory Disqus:
9.7k 9 mins.

I remembered hearing many negative complaints from my friends who had stayed in famous consulting firms, Bain, BCG, just count a few. Those firms set up a high level for entry (target school, high GPA, rich internship experience), yet assigned these "god's favored" candidates with tasks that could be easily handled by high-school students. "Just like an advanced sweatshop", said one of my friends. I have to admit that this argument is kind of exaggerated — I believe that not every student holding a high-school diploma can be capable of those works. But that's the point. It's quite natural — someone is more capable than others, no matter educated or not.

But then, hold on, why do we get an education? To narrow down the discussion area, I am not going to talk about educationalists' opinions. Things like '"Education is a tree shaking a tree, a cloud to promote a cloud, a soul awaken another soul...", well, quite noble, but I am not so interested in that. I wonder some understandable explanations that are more down to earth. Of course, now it's the turn of economists. In an economic context, we are always concerned with the "market". For our discussion in education, the "job market" is the key research object. Though a sad fact, most people get an education to ensure future living -- getting a job and money after education is the first priority.

In economics, the classical view regarding the purpose of education is quite intuitive. That is, education can enhance our "ability" to work. To be more specific, compared to a high-school diploma holder, a graduate from the university will have a higher level of competence and therefore deserve a higher level of salary. But the problem is, "ability" is a thing hard to measure. Does the so-called "ability improvement" really exist? Honestly speaking, not always. Just go back to our beginning example about the consulting firms, you would realize two facts. First, university education may not improve our ability at all. The second, someone "can" implies "always can"; and someone "cannot" implies "always cannot".

With the two cold hard facts, we are making the very first step into the world of modern economics. In 1973, Spence proposed a brand new theory to identify the role of education in the job market. He argued that worker's ability is instinctive while education can do nothing on that. Workers of higher ability get an education to distinguish themselves from those workers of lower ability. In a more formal word, they send "signal" to the company, saying, "look, I am the one of high ability!"

Yes, as you may discover, the educated one knows that education won't raise her or his ability, while on the opposite, will bring some additional educational costs, but they still choose to do so. I know, it seems a bit counter-intuitive. Anyway, let me leave suspense here. Now I will set up the model step by step to illustrate the idea.

Education as a "Signaling" device

For simplicity, let us consider a job market only consisting of two types of workers, the one of high ability, the one of low ability (you can always do this by clustering). Let \(\theta\) denote the ability of workers. For high ability workers, \(\theta=H\); for low ability workers, \(\theta=L\). Of course, \(H>L>0\). As the job market is a mixture of these two types of workers under our assumptions, we may further assume the fraction of high ability worker is \(\lambda\in(0,1)\). In a more extreme setting, we regard this \(\lambda\) as a probability determined by nature.

\[P(the\ worker\ is \ of \ type\ H )=P(\theta=H)=\lambda\]

\[P(the\ worker\ is \ of \ type\ L )=P(\theta=L)=1-\lambda\]

Now comes the most important term in this theory. The worker's type is private information to herself. The worker knows her natural endowments and chooses the level of education \(e\). For the company, it's impossible to observe the actual ability type of a worker, but it can observe her education level. Here, "worker's type as a private information" causes an information asymmetry, which is the key element in our signaling game. Always keep in mind, education is served as a signaling device.

However, education is costly. So we construct an education cost function \(c(e,\theta)\) for a worker, which is dependent on her ability and chosen education level. For the sake of analysis, we assume \(c(e,\theta)\) is twice continuously differentiable, and

  • For education level \(e\):

    • \(c(0,\theta)=0\): no education, no cost.

    • \(c_e(e,\theta)>0\): more education, more cost.

      (e.g. High school is more costly than kindergarten.)

    • \(c_{ee}(e,\theta)\geq 0\): more education, more marginal cost.

      (e.g. Consider two cases. If you are a high school student that is intended to go into a college, or if you are a kindergarten child that is planned to get into a primary school, the former one would bring more additional cost.)

  • For worker's ability \(\theta\):

    • \(c_{\theta}(e,\theta)<0\): higher ability, less cost. (So \(c(e,H)<c(e,L),\ \forall e\))
    • \(c_{e\theta}(e,\theta)<0\): higher ability, less marginal cost. (So \(c_e(e,H)<c_e(e,L),\ \forall e\))

Again, for simplicity, we suppose there are two firms hiring workers on the market. After observing the education level of the two workers, they simultaneously offer two wages \(w=w_1\ or\ w=w_2\) for the workers to choose. Certainly, the workers will choose the higher one.

In economics analysis, each agent wants to maximize her expected payoff, which is fuction characterized as below.

  • Firms: \(v(w,e|\theta)=\mathbb{E}(\theta|e)-w\)

    • Firm's expected payoff is it's gain, the expectation of worker's ability \(\theta\) after observing the education level \(e\) minus its expenditure, wage \(w\).

  • Workers: \(u(w,e|\theta)=w-c(e,\theta),\ \theta\in \{H,L\}\)

    • Worker's expected payoff is the wage she receives minus her former eduction cost.

    If we plot the level map of function \(u(w,e)\) for \(\theta=H,L\), we may discover a cross in the graph of type H and type L. How to prove it? Very simple!

​ For each level curve, i.e. for a fixed \(\theta\), \(u(w,e)=\bar u\) is a constant. Then \[ w=c(e,\theta)+constant \] As we've already had \(0<c_e(e,H)<c_e(e,L),\ \forall e\), we know that at \((w^*,e^*)\), type L is of steeper slope than type H, which is called as single crossing property.

To be more concrete, we introduce a nice candidate for the education cost function (it satisfied the property above, you may have a check): \[ c(e,\theta)=\frac{e}{\theta},\ \theta\in \{H,L\} \]

For the firms, worker's ability reflects her productivity. In equilibrium, each firm will offer wage=ability. Why? Think like a hiring manager in the firm. There is always another firm (your opponent firm) that wants to snatch workers from you. If you offer a wage lower than a worker's ability, your opponent can set a wage with a slightly more amount than you, and win all the workers. On the other hand, if you offer a wage higher than a worker's ability, you will lose money as the worker's productivity cannot compensate the wage you pay for her. In short, in equilibrium, firms make zero profit. Don't be so surprised -- it usually happens in a completely competitive market.

It's easy to figure out, there are two kinds of equilibria. The first one we called separating equilibria, is the occasion when two types of workers choose different levels of education. The second one we called pooling equilibria, refers to when two types of workers choose the same level of education.

Separating equilibria

First we are going to analyze the separating equilibria.

  • Worker's strategy: only high ability worker chooses get educated. \[ e_L=0,\ e_H=e^*>0 \]

  • Firm's belief: after observing \(e\), firm believes
    • a worker is of type H with probability 1 if \(e\geq e^*\)
    • a worker is of type L with probability 1 if \(e<e^*\)
  • Firm's strategy: with above beliefs, firm offers a wage
    • \(w_H=H\) to a worker is of type H
    • \(w_L=L\) to a worker is of type L

We claim: the situation above is a separating equilibria if

  1. Type L workers won't deviate from choosing \(e_L=0\) (the payoff from choosing \(e_L=0\) is greater than choosing \(e_L=e^*\) ), i.e. \[w_L-c(0,L)\geq w_H-c(e^*,L)\] \[L-0\geq H-e^*/L\Rightarrow e^*\geq L(H-L)\]

    This requires \(e^*\) for receiving high wage cannot be too low. Otherwise, type L workers will get an education and mimic type H.

  2. Type H workers won't deviate from choosing \(e_H=e^*\) (the payoff from choosing \(e_H=e^*\) is greater than choosing \(e_H=0\) ), i.e. \[w_H-c(e^*,H)\geq w_L-c(0,H)\] \[H-e^*/H\geq L-0\Rightarrow e^*\leq H(H-L)\]

    This requires \(e^*\) for receiving high wage cannot be too high. Otherwise, type H workers will not get an education and mimic type L.

Now we obtained the restriction for \(e^*\): \[ L(H-L)\leq e^*\leq H(H-L) \]

Note that \(e^*>0\), we have an efficiency loss -- high ability workers must get an education (with education cost) to distinguish themselves from those of low ability. Imagine when information is symmetric, the firms know high ability workers are of high ability, so offer them with high wage directly. There is no need for these high-ability workers to get additional education, as it will only bring costs. That is, ** high-ability workers are worse off due to information asymmetry**.

Pooling equilibria

Next we are going to talk about the pooling equilibria.

  • Worker's strategy: \(e_H=e_L=e^*\geq0\)
  • Firm's belief: after observing \(e\), firms believe
    • if \(e\geq e^*\):
      • the worker is of type H with probability \(\lambda\)
      • the worker is of type L with probability \(1-\lambda\)
    • if \(e<e^*\): the worker is of type L with probability 1
  • Firm's strategy: with above beliefs, firm offers a wage
    • \(w^*=\lambda H+(1-\lambda)L\) to a worker if \(e\geq e^*\)
    • \(w_L=L\) to a worker if \(e<e^*\)

We claim: the situation above is a pooling equilibria if

  1. Type L workers won't deviate from choosing \(e_L=e^*\) (the payoff from choosing \(e_L=e^*\) is greater than choosing \(e_L=\) 0), i.e. \[ \lambda H+(1-\lambda)L-e^*/L\geq L\Rightarrow e^*\leq \lambda L(H-L) \]

  2. Type H workers won't deviate from choosing \(e_H=e^*\) (the payoff from choosing \(e_H=e^*\) is greater than choosing \(e_H=0\) ), i.e. \[ \lambda H+(1-\lambda)L-e^*/H\geq L\Rightarrow e^*\leq \lambda H(H-L) \]

Now we obtained the restriction for \(e^*\): \[ 0\leq e^*\leq \lambda L(H-L) \]

Note that when \(e^*>0\), we have an efficiency loss, just like discussion in the separating equilibrium. But also, if \(e^*=0\), we can reach the only Pareto efficient outcome in this setup of asymmetric information.

In the real world, the education level \(e\) may not only refer to the quantity of education (if you are a Bachelor or a Master) but largely depend on the quality of education (if you are from a top university or not). With an appropriate size of \(\lambda\), high-ability workers always need to distinguish themselves from others, and the only way is to get more education from a top university.

This model reveals a sort of astonishing fact: students who spend decades in school may just want to signal themselves as productive rather than actually becoming more productive. People who don't go to school choose to be so because education is too costly for their natural endowments.

Now, back to our first example of consulting firms again, do you have some new ideas?
